Multimodality

The idea of writing has changed in recent years- or should I say, the idea of communication has changed in recent years. According to guidebook authors Kristin Arola, Cheryl Ball and Jennifer Sheppard, there has been a shift "from a composition pedagogy focused exclusively on the written word to a pedagogy that includes a range of semiotic modes, including (but not limited to) oral, written, and visual communication." (Arola et. al)

Reflection Question 1:

... That doesn't necessarily mean teaching many different, separate modes

In today's day and age, expanding the idea of teaching writing to this broader scope doesn't just imply that it's advantageous to teach about these many different modes of communication separatesly. In fact, learning in this way would not only help students convey their ideas via multiple modes of communication, it would also help them select an appropriate mode of communication to use.

Teachers of rhetoric, not teachers of writing.

As mentioned in the article, instructors are now being trained more as teachers of not just writing as a single medium through which we can communicate, but rather a variety of "integrated modes based on audience, purpose, and context" (Arola et. al). In other words, instructors are now trained to be teachers of not just writing, but of rhetoric as well.

This isn't to say, however, that such ideas haven't been brought up before. An example of a group that toyed with this idea in the past is the New London Group (more information to the right)- who subscribed to this idea long before the advent of the instant communication we enjoy with today's internet infrastructure.

The New London Group: Early Adopters

quoted from Arola et. Al
Those who drank the kool aid, as it were, of multimodal composition tend to agree, whether implicitly or explicitly, with the New London Group (NLG)—a group of literacy scholars who greatly influenced the design of our Revisions course. The NLG could be seen as spearheading the multimodal composition turn in 1996. Their deceptively simple argument that “literacy pedagogy must now account for the burgeoning variety of text forms associated with information and multimedia technologies” essentially makes a case that such pedagogies more richly prepare students for the diverse rhetorical and communicative practices they need to succeed as students, as professionals, and as citizens in the 21st century.

Multimodal Teaching in the 388V Classroom

At its core, multimodal teaching is very similar to regular writing instruction; new mediums of communication that surface over time have allowed us to effectively convey ideas in ways other than the age-old method of writing. That being said, it is not difficult to touch up the approach that 388V students take to teach the ENGL101 curriculum to make it more multimodality-friendly.

By placing more focus on mediums that aren't the classic double spaced, Times New Roman academic 'paper' that college writing courses are known for, the benefits that a writing course can have on its students are multiplied manyfold.

Example 1: The Digital Forum

The digital forum is an assignment that's a perfect example of integrating multimodality instruction into our ENGL101 curriculum. Although there is a clear presence of multimodality in the final product, such as image choice, text placement, color selection, and more, the assignment is still based on writing.

By providing students with a major assignment that allows them to convey ideas in a more experimental way, the course allows for students to put as much effort into this new type of assignment as they would for another paper. As such, they end up working equally hard to convey their ideas in this new, multimodal format, as they would for a classic college paper. By placing so much grade weightage on this new type of assignment, students have no choice but to make an effort to convey their ideas in new ways.

Example 2: The Public Remediation

The public remediation is another opportunity for students to see that there are more ways to effectively communicate ideas other than a classic 'college paper'. At the end of the day, that's what it means to properly use multimodal communication.

This is a unique assignment in that it allows students to pick a medium of communication of their own choice, which gives students the chance to try different ways to communicate. Since this is the first time this assignment is being implemented (at the time of writing), there are no past examples to go off of. I predict, however, that students will test the waters by taking a few different approaches to this assignment before selecting their medium of choice. I believe giving them the added flexibility to not only pick what they're trying to convey but also how they will be conveying it is the key to teaching good multimodal communication.

Overall, I believe these two assignments are a step in the right direction. I feel that although multimodal education is important, especially with present-day society's fixation on social media, college writing curriculum should still place an appropriate amount of focus on the classic 'college paper'. As such, writing (and writing instruction) in the digital age should definitely teach on the topic of multimodal communication, but this doesn't necessarily imply entire curriculums require overhauls. An excellent approach is to integrate assignments such as the Digital Forum & Public Remediation as ways to bridge the gap between what sort of 'writing' students consume in their everyday lives, and what they produce in college.

Section B:
Social Media & Writing in the Digital Age

This section serves to highlight the role and importance of social media in writing today. What started as a rudimentary way to communicate online has since evolved into a complex, nuanced environment through which ideas as exchanged, cultivated, and challenged everday. As such, social media is a major aspect of communication, and consequently, writing, in the digital age, and it should therefore play a part in modern-day writing instruction.

A Social Network as a Learning Network

At its very core, a social network is built to share information. The article 'A Social Network Can Be a Learning Network' describes how social networks can similarly be leveraged to share student writing, and the positive effects that this can have on their education.

The article also touches upon how students can very easily find resources that they want to draw from. In today's day and age, students like us have gotten accustomed to browsing the web, and thus, we've picked up a variety of ways to pull the information that we need from the internet.

Some may argue that Social Media has allowed students to run amok, consuming fake news, being enveloped by distractions, and wasting their time looking for pointless validation. However, this article brings to light some of the overwhelming benefits of social media in our academic world.

Students are finding increasingly innovative ways to put social media to work in producing meaningful academic discourse. Our job as instructors is to extract those phenomena that are conducive to education and emphasize them in the classroom; we should take initiative and reward productive behavior in this new environment.

Back Channel Conversations

An excellent example of a phenomenon that educators such as ourselves should help cultivate would be the Back Channel Conversation, as explained by the Chronicle in this quote:

Back channels. These days it's common for academic conferences to have back-channel conversations. While keynote speakers and session leaders are speaking, audience members are sharing highlights, asking questions, and conversing with colleagues on Twitter. Some might see this as distracting, but for many it's a form of public note-taking and active listening.

What about in the classroom?

Social media can also play a role in the classroom, as you'll find in the second article in the 'recommended reading' section. We have examined the role it plays in education from a much broader point of view, but let's take a closer look at Social Media within the college classroom.

Social Media & The Internet

The article mentions how we have a plethora of social media applications at our fingertips. Since we've grown up with these mediums, we've learned how to properly use them to our advantage for a variety of purposes. It's taken a while, but social media is beginning to play a bigger role in formal education, and we have a variety of factors to thank for that, ranging from the advent of fully online classes to the sheer accessibility of the internet in the past decade.

Students and Social Media

Before we delve into how exactly we can leverage social media for students in the college classroom, we should acknowledge that Social Media plays a massive part in the lives of students in the first place.

First and foremost, students are always connected to their smartphones. As students ourselves, we know firsthand that we depend on our smartphones for a vast portion of our information, whether it be academic or not. Here, we will focus on the college-related uses of social media.

According to the IDE Journal, a large portion of students leverage social media to communicate with faculty, complete homework assignments, plan study groups, and learn about campus activities. In addition, due to the majority of college students having smartphones today, social media has become an arguably integral part of how students form valuable interpersonal relationships as well.

Consequences of Social Media

Students seem to want more shared learning experiences from their professors thanks to their deeper involvement with social media. According to the IDE journal, their interconnectivity has resulted in more desires to learn from their peers rather than experts, along with more desires not to just memorize information, but rather to understand where to look for the information they need.

As instructors, we have to recognize these effects that social media and the internet are having on our generation of students, and only by making the necessary changes to our curriculum to reflect what our students want, can we improve our curriculum for the better.

The Twitter Essay: Embracing New Writing Standards

Now more than ever, it's time for us educators to take into account what exactly our students are consuming on a daily basis, and adjust our instruction accordingly. The article, "The Twitter Essay", describes how students are much more comfortable writing texts and twitter posts, but find academic papers to be a much more daunting task, when in actuality, both are just ways to convey ideas at the end of the day.

This goes back to our central theme of changing education to fit the needs of current students; we have to analyze the types of writing that students are most comfortable with, and build off of that when designing curriculum that will truly resonate with them.

Write Less, Say More

Knowing this, it's important to consider the importance of writing short pieces in today's day and age. Our limitless access to information is a double bladed sword; it's also reduced our attention spans to the point where we don't really stick with any piece of writing that we deem 'too long'. In that sense, our society has placed a value upon brevity that we haven't really seen before.

This mindset has been furthered by the nature of social media. For example, until recently, Twitter limited its users to 140 characters per each tweet; increasing it to a meager 280 over the past few years. After spending so much time creating short Facebook status updates, Instagram posts, Tweets, and Snapchats, our generation has developed and practiced the unique skill of fitting as much information as one possibly can into a limited amount of characters.

Social Media in the ENGL101 Classroom

Social media is unique, in that most curriculums don't fully address it. After all, it's very difficult to encourage the use of something that can end up being so distracting in a learning environment. Many teachers and professors that I've had have simply rejected the use of social media in the classroom altogether, citing its detrimental effects on a learning environment to be too great to come close to being outweighed by the benefits.

However, I feel that there are ways to integrate social media into English curriculum without encouraging the use of social media in the classroom outright.

For example, we can allow students to display their aptitude for social media based communication with assignments that are reminiscent of social media. I believe effective ways we could provide this for students in ENGL101 would be like asking them to write witty Twitter posts that recap a current event, or to create a social media campaign for a certain cause that they care about (one of the many promising options that students can select in the remediation assignment). I feel like a great way to introduce these would be to provide them within the Public Remediation assignment, and as extensions to the Digital Forum.

The Idea of Digital Writing

In order to learn more about teaching writing in the digital age, we should define digital writing. Fortunately, we have a fairly solid definition given to us in the first article in the recommened reading.

Digital writing, Kajder said, “is anything that involves the computer or some type of digital tool that’s going to allow you to hop online.”

Digital writing is a fairly broad topic, and in that sense, we has educators have an obligation to give students an option to explore the vast array of digital writing options that are available to them. Again, as mentioned in the quote to the left, digital writing can range from anywhere to a quick snapchat picture posted on your story, to a lengthy, heartfelt Facebook post, to anywhere in between. It's a new type of communication that we can, with some adjustment to our curriculae, allow students to accumulate a great amount of practice with.

It's important to add that this is not a contained phenomenon. Digital writing is something that's taking place on a large scale, and college students just so happen to be a sizable portion of that network.

Kajder's "Literacy Footprints" & Student Web Usage

When Miss Kajder, the English Faculty member who provided the previously listed quote, tasked her students with actually tracking their web usage in order to obtain a 'literacy footprint', she was met with interesting results.

She had an idea that students were communicating with those in their own classes, in groups of '30 to 60'. What she didn't expect, was that students (at Pitt) were communicating with hundreds of others, working collaboratively to produce a variety of mediums through which they conveyed information, ranging from wikis to documents to infographics.

For the majority of this analysis, I have been advocating for rapid restructuring of certain portions of the ENGL101 curriculum, citing a need to move towards a more digital-friendly format. However, according to Miss Kajder, this may not be the best course of action.

Her first goal was to recognize that she shouldn’t change every assignment, but should start out with one per term. “I was going to start small and scale it up from that point and make sure that that worked well.”

Here, we can draw a parallel between Miss Kajder's advice and our own English curriculum. By slowly and gradually implementing assignments such as the Digital Forum & the Public Remediation, we can test the waters and iteratively develop these assignments so that they provide the maximum benefit to our students.

She also acknowledges the public audience in the same way that we acknowledge it while presenting our assignments.

those projects and pieces of work can have greater meaning and they actually become much more purposeful outside of the classroom space

It's important to note that, unlike a simple paper that will just be turned in, graded, and returned, this new type of published, publicly available product will be available to the rest of the web, to be used as a resource (as we explored earlier with the example in Section A).

Her experience as a teacher and advice will surely be useful as we slowly but surely adjust our ENGL101 curriculum to suit the needs of increasingly digitally involved freshman class.

Unique Perspective: Rights of the American People

Without a doubt, we as the American People enjoy a variety of liberties. With how easy it's become to share information with the world in today's day and age, it was just a matter of time before proposed legal framework was erected to maintain order and fairness within this new environment.

If you'll refer to recommended reading article 2, you'll find a brave group of individuals looking to provide a bill of rights for digital learners. This pertains to our curriculum, as we are educators. As our roles transition from traditional writing instructors to digital writing/communication instructors to fit the era, we will be training the minds of tomorrow in the distribution of the wealth of information that they have to the general public.

In that sense, I feel that it is just to ingrain these students with a sense of righteousness; in order to keep them ethically informed about the power they hold as educated distributors of information throught their own writing in the digital age.

The article explores the provision of basic 'rights' to the people, including the right to access, privacy, public knowledge, and even financial transparency.

It is important to note that given how easy it is to share information today, there is without a doubt plenty of room for exploitation of the masses. I believe that the main takeaway from this proposed Bill of Rights is that we should imbue our writing students with a certain sense of integrity, so that they use their knowledge and ultimately, their ability to share information in an ethical manner.

Digital Literacy and Wisdom

The internet can be used for many things, but we will focus on it here as a medium for productive discourse.

My understanding is that, like what's described in recommended readings #3 and #4, there's a certain type of wisdom and literacy that we have to achieve and maintain in order to properly leverage this new and exciting resource.

In general, we have to teach ourselves to interact online in the same way that we would politely interact in real life. Sometimes, the guise of anonymity might enable us to become more caustic; the instant response time and lack of foreseeable consequences may enable us to make logical mistakes. These, and all other quirks that accompany writing in the digital age are phenomena that we should recognize and deal with accordingly if we are to become successful writers in the digital age.

Teaching Writing in the Digital Age & the ENGL101 Classroom

I would like to present a few tools here that Miss Sara Kajder from recommended reading #1 suggested that I believe would be helpful in enhancing our current ENGL101 curriculum.

Videos: a New Way to Communicate

Videos are a fairly unexplored format for English classes, given that they differ so much from the classic English essay. However, we can't forget that the majority of the content today's students consume is probably from YouTube (and other streaming services) alone. The website is home to a variety of entertainment and lifestyle channels, but also facilitates a great many news personalities and educators: people who provide news and opinions in a format not unlike the writing that we put students to today.

I believe that this format is worth exploring, and the tools that Miss Kajder suggests are a great way to break into that medium, at least, from a technical point of view.

  • Video Ant is a video annotation and mockup service that allows students to annotate videos in the same way that we have annotated textbook pages, articles, and essays. Videos are great informational means in their own right, and I believe it's about time we started providing formal instruction to students regarding how to annotate this way to communicate.

  • WeVideo is a video creation service that doesn't require any downloads. Unlike software such as IMovie or Windows Movie Maker, it's fully broswer based, making it much more accessible to all sorts of college students. I feel that it's equally important to have a means to create videos within the college classroom if you are going to be analyzing them, so I've included this selection of Miss Kajder as well.

In conclusion, teaching writing in the digital age is a difficult task. The appearance of new mediums that we haven't yet developed a lot of tried-and-true curriculum for has left us at at interesting crossroads. English curriculum can either adapt to encompass all these new ways to convey information, or it can remain as it always has, and risk being antiquated.

I believe it is our job as educators to recognize that teaching writing in the digital age means that we will have to make significant changes to our curriculum.
